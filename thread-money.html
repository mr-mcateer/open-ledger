Thread 01 — Follow the Money

The Money Trail

Iranian intelligence funds → Ivan Boesky → L.F. Rothschild → Drexel Burnham Lambert → Crédit Lyonnais → Robert Maxwell → Apollo Global → Epstein

How did a French bank connected to a dead spy's 600 shell companies provide the founding capital for the world's largest private equity firm? Follow the money.

The Banker from Iran

In the early 1960s, Ivan Boesky lived in Iran and built ties to a powerful family there. He came back to the U.S. with money nobody could explain — no family wealth, no business wins, no inheritance.

Without even a college degree, Boesky landed a job at L.F. Rothschild, a top New York brokerage firm. Wall Street firms checked your background obsessively — so someone important opened the door for him. Within years, he became one of the biggest stock traders alive.

The Daily Calls

Every trading day, Boesky called Michael Milken at Drexel Burnham Lambert. These were not friendly chats. Boesky was a pipeline — moving suspicious money into Milken's pool of investors and deals.

Boesky's office was in the Pahlavi Foundation building in New York — a property owned by the Iranian government. A Wall Street stock trader, working out of an Iranian government building, calling the king of junk bonds — risky loans that pay huge returns — every single day.

The Junk Bond Machine

Michael Milken at Drexel Burnham Lambert basically invented the market for junk bonds — risky loans that pay huge returns. His Beverly Hills office became the most powerful money machine in America, funding hostile corporate takeovers that reshaped the Fortune 500.

Working under Milken was Leon Black. Black invented the "highly confident letter" — a written promise from Drexel's billionaire network saying "we have the money to buy your company." It was a financial weapon: one piece of paper that could move billions.

Think of it like this: Imagine writing an IOU so scary that companies surrendered before you even paid.

The Collapse

In 1988, prosecutor Rudy Giuliani charged Milken with crimes no financier had ever faced. Drexel collapsed by 1990. Its huge pile of junk bonds was sold off at pennies on the dollar — marked as nearly worthless on paper.

But Leon Black had spent years inside the machine. He knew exactly which "worthless" assets would actually recover. Everyone else saw wreckage. Black saw a shopping list.

Think of it like this: A store burns down and everything is marked "damaged." But one employee knows which boxes are fine inside.

The Phone Call

In 1990, while Drexel's ashes were still warm, Leon Black got a call from Crédit Lyonnais — a French government-owned bank. They offered him $4 billion to start a fund buying up cheap, beaten-down debt — the exact assets Black knew were underpriced.

Black flew to Paris and said the number was wrong: "You have to give me $8 billion." They agreed. Apollo Global Management was born — funded almost entirely by one French bank with deep ties to spy agencies across Europe and the Middle East.

Think of it like this: A stranger hands you $8 billion to buy things only you know the real price of. Why you?

The 600 Shell Companies

Robert Maxwell — Ghislaine Maxwell's father — ran about 600 shell companies. A shell company is a business that exists only on paper. Their main job: hiding KGB and Soviet money offshore as the USSR fell apart. Billions needed to be cleaned and moved before the old system collapsed.

Crédit Lyonnais was the bank at the center of it. Maxwell and the bank were tangled together through shared accounts, loans, and shadowy corporate structures. Maxwell died mysteriously in November 1991, falling off his yacht into the Atlantic.

Jeffrey Epstein's personal wealth exploded starting in February 1992 — three months after Maxwell's death. Where did that money come from?

The Fire

In May 1996, the headquarters of Crédit Lyonnais in Paris burned to the ground. The fire started in the computer room — the exact room that stored the bank's central records.

French investigators had been closing in on billions in suspicious transactions. After the fire, roughly $20 billion could never be accounted for. The records that could have traced money from Maxwell's shell companies through Apollo and beyond were gone.

Think of it like this: Imagine the IRS is about to audit you, and your filing cabinet catches fire the week before.

The $170 Million

Epstein had been building a relationship with Leon Black since the 1980s. Black eventually paid Epstein about $170 million over five years — supposedly for "estate planning" and "tax advice." No financial advisor in history has ever been paid that much for such work.

In January 2026, DOJ files revealed something bigger: Epstein had power of attorney — legal permission to move Black's money through Deutsche Bank accounts. Epstein was not just giving advice. He could spend Black's money himself.

The Chain

Put together, these records trace one money pipeline across four decades and three continents — ending at the founding of the world's largest private investment firm.

Apollo Global Management's startup money did not come from clean sources. It traces through a burned French bank, a dead spy's 600 shell companies, and a money pipeline that starts with intelligence-linked finance in the 1960s.
