How did a mysterious French bank — connected to a dead spy's 600 fake companies — provide the startup money for the world's largest investment firm? This trail crosses four decades and three continents. Follow the money.

Early 1960s · The Cold War is at its peak. Intelligence agencies are recruiting financial operatives worldwide.

The Banker from Iran

In the early 1960s, a man named Ivan Boesky lived in Iran and built ties to one of the country's most powerful families. He came back to the U.S. with money nobody could explain — no family wealth, no business success, no inheritance. Just cash.

Without even a college degree, Boesky landed a job at L.F. Rothschild, one of the top stock trading firms on Wall Street. These firms background-checked everyone obsessively — so someone very powerful opened the door for him. Within a few years, he became one of the biggest stock traders in the world.

A guy with no degree and no explained wealth walks out of Iran and into one of the most exclusive clubs on Wall Street. That doesn't happen by accident. Someone opened that door.

Why does this matter to you? Because the money pipeline that starts here ends at the firm that manages pension funds — possibly including yours.

1970s–1980s · Iran's Shah falls in 1979. The Pahlavi Foundation's U.S. assets remain in place.

The Daily Calls

Every single trading day, Boesky called Michael Milken at a firm called Drexel Burnham Lambert. These weren't friendly chats. Boesky was a pipeline — funneling suspicious money into Milken's pool of investors and deals.

Here's the strange part: Boesky's office was in the Pahlavi Foundation building in New York — a property literally owned by the Iranian government. Picture this: one of Wall Street's biggest traders, working out of an Iranian government building, calling the most powerful bond dealer in America every single day. Nobody asked questions.

An Iranian-connected trader with unexplained money, calling a junk bond king every day, from an Iranian government building in Manhattan. This is where the money trail begins.

1980s · While Americans celebrate a booming stock market, Milken's junk bonds are funding the hostile takeovers behind it.

The Junk Bond Machine

Michael Milken at Drexel Burnham Lambert basically invented the market for "junk bonds." Here's what that means: normal bonds are safe loans to reliable companies. Junk bonds are risky loans to shaky companies — they might fail, but if they don't, you make a fortune. Milken's Beverly Hills office became the most powerful money machine in America.

Working under Milken was a man named Leon Black. Remember that name — he's the reason this story matters today. Black invented something called the "highly confident letter" — basically a written promise from Drexel's network of billionaires saying "we have the money to buy your company." One piece of paper that could force a company worth billions to surrender.

Think of it like this: imagine writing an IOU so intimidating that companies gave up before you even paid. That was Leon Black's invention.

1988–1990 · While Giuliani gets famous for the prosecution, watch where the assets go.

The Collapse

In 1988, prosecutor Rudy Giuliani charged Milken with crimes no financier had ever faced. Drexel collapsed by 1990. Its massive pile of junk bonds got sold off at pennies on the dollar — marked as nearly worthless on paper.

But here's the key: Leon Black had spent years inside the machine. He knew which "worthless" assets were actually going to recover in value. Everyone else looked at Drexel's wreckage and saw garbage. Black looked at the same pile and saw a shopping list.

Think of it like this: a store burns down and the insurance company marks everything "damaged — sell for almost nothing." But one employee who worked there knows which boxes are perfectly fine inside. He buys them all for pennies. That's what Leon Black did.

1990 · The Berlin Wall fell last year. Billions in Soviet-bloc money need new homes.

The Phone Call

In 1990, while Drexel's ashes were still warm, Leon Black got a phone call that changed everything. Crédit Lyonnais — a bank owned by the French government — offered him $4 billion to start a fund buying up cheap, beaten-down debt. The exact assets Black knew were secretly underpriced.

Black flew to Paris and told them the number was wrong: "You have to give me $8 billion." They said yes. Just like that, Apollo Global Management was born — one of the largest investment firms in history, funded almost entirely by a single French government bank with deep ties to spy agencies across Europe and the Middle East.

Think of it like this: a stranger calls you out of nowhere and hands you $8 billion to buy things only you know the real price of. No bidding process. No competition. Just you. Why?

Crédit Lyonnais = a bank owned by the French government. Not a private company — a state-run bank with deep ties to intelligence agencies across Europe and the Middle East. This is where the next part gets dark.

1991 · The USSR collapses in December. Billions in state assets are looted. The money needs somewhere to go.

The 600 Shell Companies

Robert Maxwell — Ghislaine Maxwell's father — ran roughly 600 shell companies. What's a shell company? It's a business that exists only on paper — no employees, no office, no product. It's a mailbox for moving money. Investigators found Maxwell used these to move billions offshore as the Soviet Union collapsed. Dirty money needed to be cleaned and hidden before the old system fell apart.

And the bank at the center of Maxwell's empire? Crédit Lyonnais — the same French bank that just gave Leon Black $8 billion. Maxwell and the bank were tangled together through shared accounts, loans, and shadowy corporate structures that investigators are still trying to untangle.

Maxwell died mysteriously in November 1991, falling off his yacht into the Atlantic Ocean. Three months later, in February 1992, Jeffrey Epstein's personal wealth suddenly exploded. Where did that money come from?

A spy with 600 fake companies dies. Three months later, Epstein suddenly gets rich. Both are connected to the same bank — the bank that just gave Leon Black $8 billion. That's not a coincidence. That's a trail.

May 1996 · French investigators are closing in. Then the records disappear.

The Fire

In May 1996, the headquarters of Crédit Lyonnais in Paris burned to the ground. The fire started in one specific room: the computer room — the exact room that stored the bank's central financial records.

French investigators had been closing in on billions in suspicious transactions. After the fire, roughly $20 billion could never be accounted for. The records that could have traced money from Maxwell's 600 shell companies, through Apollo, and beyond — gone. Up in smoke.

Think of it like this: imagine the IRS is about to audit you, and the one filing cabinet with all your records catches fire the week before. Convenient, right?

$20 billion — gone. The records that could have connected Maxwell's fake companies, Crédit Lyonnais, Apollo's founding money, and Epstein — all destroyed in one fire, in one room, right as investigators were closing in.

2012–2017 · While you're recovering from the 2008 crash, $170M flows from a billionaire to a sex trafficker.

The $170 Million

Epstein had been building a relationship with Leon Black — Apollo's founder — since the 1980s. Over five years, Black paid Epstein roughly $170 million. The official reason? "Estate planning" and "tax advice." No financial advisor in the history of the profession has ever been paid anywhere near that much for such work.

Then in January 2026, DOJ files revealed something even bigger: Epstein had power of attorney over Black's accounts.

Power of attorney = legal permission to move someone else's money for them. You sign a piece of paper and the other person can write checks, move funds, and make transactions using your accounts — without asking you each time.

Epstein wasn't just giving advice — he could spend Leon Black's money himself, through Deutsche Bank accounts, with no one looking over his shoulder.

Apollo manages over $600 billion today — including pension funds, retirement accounts, and insurance policies that belong to ordinary Americans. The firm's founder paid $170 million to a convicted sex trafficker and gave him the keys to his bank accounts. That's whose hands your retirement money passes through.
