Thread 01 — Follow the Money

The Money Trail

Iranian intelligence funds → Ivan Boesky → L.F. Rothschild → Drexel Burnham Lambert → Crédit Lyonnais → Robert Maxwell → Apollo Global → Epstein

How did a French bank connected to a dead spy's 600 shell companies provide the founding capital for the world's largest private equity firm? Follow the money.

The Banker from Iran

In the early 1960s, Ivan Boesky lived in Iran, where he developed a close friendship with an Iranian national whose family had significant connections. He returned to the United States with wealth that had no clear origin story -- no family fortune, no prior business success, no inheritance on the record.

Despite lacking an undergraduate degree, Boesky secured a position at L.F. Rothschild in New York, one of the city's established brokerage firms. In an industry that screened obsessively for pedigree, someone had vouched for him. Within years, he would become one of the most aggressive arbitrageurs on Wall Street.

The Daily Calls

Every trading day, Ivan Boesky spoke by phone with Michael Milken at Drexel Burnham Lambert. The calls were not casual. Boesky served as a conduit -- funneling money of questionable provenance into Milken's network of investors and deal pools.

Boesky maintained an office in the Pahlavi Foundation building in New York, a property controlled by the Iranian government. The building was a known hub for Iranian state-linked financial operations during and after the Shah's reign. A Wall Street arbitrageur, working out of an Iranian government building, speaking daily to the architect of the junk bond market.

The Junk Bond Machine

At Drexel Burnham Lambert, Michael Milken effectively invented the high-yield debt market. His operation in Beverly Hills became the most powerful capital-raising engine in American finance -- funding leveraged buyouts, hostile takeovers, and corporate raids that reshaped the Fortune 500.

Working under Milken was Leon Black. Black's specific contribution was the invention of the "highly confident letter" -- a promissory note from Drexel's network of billionaire investors that signaled enough capital existed to complete a hostile takeover. It was a financial weapon: a piece of paper that could move billions.

The Collapse

In 1988, Rudy Giuliani -- then U.S. Attorney for the Southern District of New York -- prosecuted Michael Milken, deploying racketeering statutes that had never been used against a financier. Drexel Burnham Lambert collapsed between 1989 and 1990, and its massive portfolio of junk bonds and distressed assets was liquidated at fire-sale prices, marked as near-worthless on paper.

But Leon Black had spent years inside the machine. He knew which assets Drexel was dumping were actually valuable -- which bonds would recover, which companies would survive. The market saw wreckage. Black saw a shopping list.

The Phone Call

In 1990, as Drexel's ashes were still smoldering, Leon Black received a call from Crédit Lyonnais, a French state-owned bank. The bank offered him $4 billion to start a new fund that would buy distressed debt -- the very assets Black knew best.

Black flew to Paris. He did not accept the offer. He told them the number was wrong: "You have to give me $8 billion." Crédit Lyonnais agreed. Apollo Global Management was born, seeded almost entirely by a single French bank with deep ties to intelligence operations across Europe and the Middle East.

The 600 Shell Companies

Robert Maxwell -- Ghislaine Maxwell's father -- had built an empire of approximately 600 shell companies. Their primary function: holding KGB and Soviet money offshore as the USSR collapsed. Billions needed to be laundered, parked, and repositioned before the old system dissolved entirely.

Crédit Lyonnais was a primary vehicle for these operations. Maxwell and the bank were deeply intertwined through shared accounts, loans, and opaque corporate structures. Maxwell died under mysterious circumstances in November 1991, falling from his yacht in the Atlantic.

Jeffrey Epstein's personal wealth, by multiple accounts, exploded starting in February 1992 -- three months after Maxwell's death. The timing raises an unavoidable question about the origin of those funds.

7

The Fire

In May 1996, the headquarters of Crédit Lyonnais on Boulevard des Italiens in Paris burned to the ground. The fire was catastrophic and targeted: it started in the computer room that housed the bank's central records.

French investigators had been closing in on the bank's losses and the network of questionable transactions flowing through its books. After the fire, approximately $20 billion in funds could never be accounted for. The records that could have traced the flow of money from Maxwell's shell companies through Apollo and beyond were destroyed.

The $170 Million

Jeffrey Epstein had targeted Leon Black since the 1980s, cultivating a relationship that deepened over decades. Eventually, Black paid Epstein approximately $170 million over five years, ostensibly for "estate planning" and "tax advice." No financial advisor in history has commanded fees on that scale for such services.

In January 2026, DOJ file releases revealed a further dimension: Epstein held power of attorney to trade on Leon Black's behalf through Deutsche Bank accounts. Epstein did not merely advise Black. He had legal authority to move Black's money.

The Chain

Laid end to end, the documented record traces a single financial pipeline across four decades, three continents, and the founding of what became the world's largest alternative asset manager.

The founding capital of Apollo Global Management does not trace to clean institutional money. It traces through a burned French bank, a dead spy's network of shell companies, and a capital pipeline that begins in intelligence-adjacent finance of the 1960s.
